Primary Science Explorer interactive
investigations for web simulation
Executive summary
Download the PDF report
1
This report specifies a curriculum-mapped catalogue of interactive experiments and investigations that
can be simulated in a web application to support “working scientifically” across primary science in
England . The mapping uses the statutory year-by-year science programmes of study for Years 1–6,
plus Reception alignment through the current EYFS “The Natural World” Early Learning Goal. 
2
The design premise is that the app should not treat “working scientifically” as a bolt-on mini-game. The
statutory guidance explicitly positions “working scientifically” as something that must be taught
through substantive science content rather than as a separate strand, with pupils learning via different
enquiry types (e.g., observing over time, pattern seeking, identifying/classifying, comparative/fair
testing, and secondary research). 
3
The catalogue contains one “anchor” interactive investigation for every curriculum area in each year
group (Years 1–6), and a Reception-aligned set based on the EYFS “Natural World” statements. Each
investigation specification includes: description, intended learning outcomes, variables, user
interactions, web feasibility notes, assessment/extension ideas, safety notes, and an estimated
development complexity (low/medium/high). 
4
Evidence from research syntheses and reviews indicates that computer simulations can improve science
learning outcomes versus some traditional approaches, but that unguided discovery can be inefficient
and error-prone without scaffolds (prompts, constraints, feedback, and opportunities to explain). 
This report therefore recommends a “guided enquiry” UX model: pupils still make decisions and run
trials, but the app structures the enquiry cycle and makes variables, data capture, repetition, and
reporting unavoidable rather than optional.

Primary statutory sources

5
The science programmes of study for Key Stages 1 and 2 define year-by-year substantive content and
the “working scientifically” expectations for KS1 (Years 1–2), lower KS2 (Years 3–4), and upper KS2 (Years

For Reception, science is not specified as a discrete national curriculum subject, so the mapping is
anchored in the EYFS statutory framework, specifically the Early Learning Goal (ELG) “The Natural
World” (exploration and observation of animals/plants; contrasting environments; seasons; and
changing states of matter).

1
The national curriculum also notes that “most of the learning about science should be done through the
use of first-hand practical experiences” while still using appropriate secondary sources (books,
photographs, videos). This matters for product positioning: the app should be framed as (a) preparation
for practical work, (b) consolidation, and (c) widening access when practical constraints exist—not as a
replacement for hands-on science.

Evidence-informed design patterns for web simulations
10
Research reviews and meta-analyses report positive learning impacts from science simulations in many
contexts, including conceptual understanding and process skills.  At the same time, classic review
evidence warns that “pure discovery” in simulation environments can lead to confusion, weak
strategies, and inefficient learning without instructional supports; the best outcomes generally emerge
when simulations are combined with scaffolding and opportunities to articulate explanations. 
6
2
A practical implication for a Primary Science Explorer app is to “productise scaffolding” so it is built into
the interaction model rather than being dependent on the teacher’s real-time intervention.
Core enquiry loop UX
flowchart TD
A[Pick year group & topic] --> B[Choose or write a question]
B --> C[Plan: what to change / measure / keep same]
C --> D[Run trial]
D --> E[Record: table + notes]
E --> F[Analyse: charts or sorting]
F --> G[Conclusion with evidence]
G --> H[Reflect: reliability + next question]
H --> I[Save/share report]
The “types of scientific enquiry” language used in UK primary practice (observing over time; pattern
seeking; identifying/classifying; comparative/fair testing; researching using secondary sources) aligns
well with this loop and can be used as a navigation taxonomy. 
11
Simulation interaction patterns by key stage
In KS1, interactions should bias toward concrete choices (icons, drag-sort, “run test” button) and
simple recording (tally marks, pictograms), reflecting the KS1 objectives around simple questions,
observations, tests, classifying, and recording. 
9
In KS2, the app can progressively introduce:- explicit variable panels (independent/dependent/controls),- repeated trials and simple averaging, and- richer data visualisation (bar/line graphs, and in UKS2 potentially scatter graphs). 
Technical feasibility notes and web standards
12
9
Most proposed simulations can be implemented with standard web platform capabilities: - 2D
interactive scenes and measurement overlays: HTML Canvas 2D is a common approach for drawing and
interaction. - Sound investigations: the Web Audio API supports synthesising tones (e.g., oscillator sources) suitable
for pitch/volume experiments with appropriate volume limiting and defaults. - Accessibility: for school-facing/public-sector-aligned products, WCAG 2.2 is a widely referenced
baseline; provide keyboard alternatives for dragging, clear focus order, and non-audio alternatives for
key information. 
13
14
Safety framing for simulated investigations
Even though simulations avoid many hazards, the app should still model good scientific practice: risk
awareness, safe choices in “real-world extension” prompts, and age-appropriate warnings (e.g., not
looking at the sun). The safety notes in this report align with UK school practice that activities requiring
PPE are often not suitable for typical primary classrooms without specialist controls, and that risk
assessment should be routine.

16
EYFS Natural World – observing animals and plants: Virtual Nature Walk Journal
Brief description: tappable interactive scenes (park/pond/woodland) where children observe and then
draw/label finds in a “journal”.
Working scientifically: KS1-1, KS1-2, KS1-4, KS1-6.
Learning outcomes: careful observation, early classification, begin recording as evidence.
Inputs/variables: scene, zoom, optional “hint” overlays.
User interactions: tap-to-inspect, draw on canvas, drag labels, simple sorting.
Feasibility notes: 2D canvas drawing and asset library; store outputs locally/cloud; light randomisation
for variety.
Assessment/extension: tally charts of finds; compare scenes; teacher view of journal artifacts.
Safety notes: digital none; real-world pairing emphasises hygiene and respectful handling.
Complexity: medium.
6
EYFS Natural World – contrasting environments: Habitat Match and Explain
Brief description: drag organisms into habitats; justify choices using simple evidence cards.
Working scientifically: KS1-1, KS1-4, KS1-5.
Learning outcomes: explain simple needs; relate visible features to environment.
Inputs/variables: habitat type; feature toggles like “fur/waxy leaves”.
User interactions: drag-drop; “Why?” reveal; sentence stems.
Feasibility notes: rule-based outcomes (lookup tables) with animations.
Assessment/extension: capture justification choices; “change one thing” challenge (add shade/water).
Safety notes: digital none; real-world extension about not moving organisms between habitats.
Complexity: low.
EYFS Natural World – seasons and change: Seasons Explorer and Weather Diary
Brief description: month slider changes daylight/weather; children log daily icons.
Working scientifically: KS1-2, KS1-5, KS1-6.
Learning outcomes: recognise seasonal patterns; record and interpret simple charts.
Inputs/variables: month/day; location preset.
User interactions: sliders; icon logging; pictograms/bar charts.
Feasibility notes: precomputed sunrise/sunset for UK; local/offline storage.
Assessment/extension: questions about longest days; links to buds/migration.
Safety notes: reinforce “don’t look at the sun” in real observations. 
Complexity: medium.
9
EYFS Natural World – changing states of matter: Melting and Freezing Kitchen Lab
Brief description: heat/cool familiar materials; record time to change.
Working scientifically: KS1-1, KS1-2, KS1-3, KS1-6.
Learning outcomes: describe change; do a simple comparative test; record outcomes.
Inputs/variables: material; temperature; container type (optional).
User interactions: thermometer slider; timer; table of results; repeat trials.
Feasibility notes: time-series state-change model + animation; small randomness.
Assessment/extension: predictions; “keep everything else the same”; insulation.
Safety notes: if mirrored physically, manage hot-water risks and follow risk assessment. 
Complexity: medium.

Living things and habitats: Microorganism and Classification Key Challenge
WS links: UKS2-1, UKS2-3, UKS2-5, UKS2-6.
Core: build keys using evidence cards; evaluate and revise groupings.
Variables: organism set; feature list; evidence pack.
Interactions: key builder; justification writing; counterexample challenges.
Tech: dataset + UI; scaffolding around “evidence supports/refutes”.
Assessment/extension: debate how new evidence changes classification.
Safety: clarify that culturing microbes needs specialist controls.
Complexity: medium.

Animals including humans: Circulatory System Pump and Pulse Investigation
WS links: UKS2-1, UKS2-2, UKS2-3, UKS2-5.
Core: vary activity intensity/duration and log pulse recovery; compare profiles.
Variables: intensity, duration, “fitness profile”, measurement noise toggle.
Interactions: data logging; line graphs; report builder; evaluate trust in results.
Tech: time-series physiology model + charts.
Assessment/extension: explain variable control and repeat readings; link to lifestyle factors carefully.
Safety: safe exercise/inclusion in physical extension.
Complexity: high.
Evolution and inheritance: Natural Selection Simulator
WS links: UKS2-1, UKS2-3, UKS2-4, UKS2-5, UKS2-6.
Core: run multiple generations; track trait frequencies; refute misconceptions like “animals choose to
adapt”.
Variables: trait distribution; environmental background; predation pressure; mutation rate.
Interactions: hypothesis → repeated runs → compare graphs → conclusion with uncertainty.
Tech: agent-based or probabilistic model; needs “repeat/average runs” to teach randomness.
Assessment/extension: critique claims using evidence; optional application contexts with careful
framing.
Safety: ensure framing avoids eugenics narratives; focus on natural variation and survival.
Complexity: high.
Light: Periscope and Reflection Ray Lab
WS links: UKS2-1, UKS2-3, UKS2-5, UKS2-6.
Core: place mirrors; trace rays; create labelled diagrams as evidence.
Variables: mirror angles; positions.
Interactions: drag mirrors; ray tracing; diagram drawing/export.
Tech: 2D ray tracer + drawing canvas.
Assessment/extension: compare multi-reflections; discuss alternative designs conceptually.
Safety: avoid shining lasers/bright light into eyes in real life.
Complexity: medium.
Electricity: Voltage, Brightness and Resistance Investigation
WS links: UKS2-1, UKS2-2, UKS2-3, UKS2-5, UKS2-6.
Core: vary cells and resistance presets; log meter readings; graph outcomes; justify.
Variables: voltage (cells), resistance, circuit layout (extension).
Interactions: build; measure; record; line graphs; explanation with evidence.
Tech: simplified DC solver with guardrails (conceptual focus); built-in meters and exports.
Assessment/extension: design a “dimmer”; justify with evidence and limitations.
Safety: physical extension reinforces low-voltage-only practice.
Complexity: high.
